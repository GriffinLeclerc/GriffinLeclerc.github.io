Experimental Analysis of the Performance and Scalability of
Network Time Security for the Network Time Protocol






At the end of 2021, I decided the focus of masters research: timing protocol performance.
Specifically, how the authentication mechanisms introduced by Network Time Security (NTS) effected the performance
of networked time transfer when compared to NTPv4.

Being familiar with the mechanisms of IEEE 1588 and 802.1AS thanks to my time at the University of New Hampshire
Interoperability Laboratory, I was able to quickly understand NTP and NTS and began research almost immediately.

An open source implementation of NTS had been previously developed by Cloudflare and was readily available. By augmenting
this implementation, known as CFNTS, with Rust's builtin benchmarking framework, Cargo Bench, we were able to measure
discrete time intervals on all machines in the secure time domain and compare their performance to unauthenticated NTP.

This study determines both the performance and scalability of NTS, so two separate experiments were developed to complete
each of these objectives individually. The first experiment utilized one client connected to one server in an unloaded
environment to measure performance of NTS. The second suite implemented a many-to-one pattern where clients transmitted an
increasing number of requests per second to a single server in order to simulate a large network and measure scalability of NTS.

After a few months, myself and my academic advisor, Doctor Radim Bartos, had completed our research work.
the "Experimental Analysis of the Performance and Scalability of Network Time Security for the Network Time Protocol".

I am pleased to announce the acceptance of this work into the International IEEE Symposium on Precision Clock Synchronization
for Measurement, Control, and Communication (ISPCS), where I will present our findings in Vienna, Austria in October 2022.

The final draft of this research work is available here.

High Performance Computing Cluster Capstone

This project’s aim was to design and construct a low cost, high performance computing
(HPC) cluster for use by University of New Hampshire researchers looking to complete
experiments involving intense computational components.

The project cluster utilizes six rack mounted servers, or nodes, reclaimed by the project’s
sponsor. These nodes were provisioned with a modern Linux operating system and networked together
to form the project’s computational cluster.

The project demonstrates it is possible to construct high performance computer clusters, using reclaimed
technology, with respectable performance at minimal cost.

The full explination of the project methodology, performance benchmarking, and conclusions can be found here.

This project was presented as part of the University of New Hampshire's Undergraduate Research Conference,
as well as submitted to the PEARC 2021 conference and presented to the Northeast Cyberteam.

UNH Tech Camp Capstone

In the summer of 2019, I was a volunteer staff member at UNH Tech Camp, a week long
summer camp for middle and high school students. The tech camp experience involves a variety of
instructors who teach students about various STEM topics. I led the Computer Science
portion, where I instructed 15 students about programming basics. I also supported the
Networking and Electrical Engineering portions of the program.

We began by familiarizing ourselves with Raspberry Pi's and Arduino. Using the Arduberry shield,
we intercepted various currents to the train set and used them to manipulate the trains.
Six sets of two relays connected to the Pi's performed the following functions:
Turning the trains on and off by disconnecting the main power to/from the track;
Increasing or decreasing the speed of the trains by changing the supplied voltage between 5 and 10 volts;
Changing direction of the trains by swapping the current to each track from + to - and vice versa;
Changing the direction of three forks by providing an electric pulse to the track's magnetic coil.
Finally, there was one Raspberry Pi deemed the Master Pi. This Pi maintained ssh connections to the
others for ease of access/calibration.

Students were required to use all of the Raspberry Pi's to complete a timing-based puzzle.

At the conclusion, students displayed their knowledge to their parents and peers
in an showcase finale. Myself and a team colleagues developed this capstone showcase.

Eagle Scout Service Project

In early October 2014, I was a Life Scout in Boy Scout Troop 292, Hooksett NH.
A few of my colleagues were underway with their Eagle Scout service projects.
These projects included construction of an ice skating hut on the shore of a local pond,
and refurbishment of a popular walking path in the town forest.

I wanted to complete a less traditional project that would immediately and directly impact others.

After some research, I contacted the executive director of the Computer Technology Assistance Corperation.
They allowed me to use their space over three working days to store, assess, and refurbish
147 computers I had collected from members of the public who donated their old systems.

Of the 147 collected, 82 were made fully functional and ready for distribution, 37 were disassembled for
functional components, and 28 were disassembled and recycled. All 82 were distributed to low income
families to support their student's educational experience.

Weather Sender

Too long had I been burdened by inaccurate or bloated weather services.
So I devised a plan, a plan in which I would send myself the weather.

Thanks to the wonderful website wttr.in,
I was able to gather weather data in json format: wttr.in/?format=j1
It was simple once I located the information I was interested in capturing.

Thanks to my phone provider for allowing me to send texts over SMTP,
I am now able to notify myself of the weather conditions each morning.

In addition to learning more about json, I learned about the use of external
configuration files and bash scripting.

I decided it best to use a configuration file for users to specify their location
and recipients, rather than having them edit the script directly.
I then wrote bash script to first generate the configuration file, request (and wait for)
the user to fill relevant information, then execute the weather sending portion of the
script using that information.

Finally, in order to protect the user's privacy, the script requires the user to
create two system environment variables to access their email address and password.
This discourages users from storing their sensitive information in plain text.
