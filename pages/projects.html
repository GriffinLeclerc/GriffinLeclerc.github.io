UNH Tech Camp Capstone

In the summer of 2019, I was a volunteer staff member at UNH Tech Camp, a week long
summer camp for middle and high school students. The tech camp experience involves various
instructors who teach students about various STEM topics. I personally led the Computer Science
portion, where I instructed 15 students about programming basics. I also supported the
Networking and Electrical Engineering portions of the program.

We began by familiarizing ourselves with Raspberry Pi's and Arduino. Using the Arduberry shield,
we intercepted various currents to the train set and used them to manipulate the trains.
Six sets of two relays connected to the Pi's performed the following functions:
Turning the trains on and off by disconencting the main power to/from the track;
Increasing or decreasing the speed of the trains by changing the supplied voltage between 5 and 10 volts;
Changing direction of the trains by swapping the current to each track from + to - and vice versa;
Changing the direction of three forks by providing an electric pulse to the track's magnetic coil;
Finally there was one Raspberry Pi deemed the Master Pi. This Pi maintained ssh connections to the
others for ease of access/calibration.

Students were required to use all of the Raspberry Pi's to complete a timing-based puzzle.

At the conclusion, students displayed their knowledge to their parents and peers
in an end-of-tech-camp-showcase. Myself and a few colleagues developed this capstone showcase.

Weather Sender

Too long had I been burdened by inaccurate or bloated weather services.
So I devised a plan, a plan in which I would send myself the weather.

Thanks to the wonderful website wttr.in,
I was able to gather weather data in json format: wttr.in/?format=j1
It was simple once I located the information I was interested in capturing.

Thanks to my phone provider for allowing me to sent texts over SMTP,
I am now able to notify myself of the weather each morning.

In addition to learning more about json, I learned about the use of external
configuration files and bash scripting.

I decided it best to use a configuration file for users to specify their location
and recipients, rather than having them edit the script directly.
I then wrote bash script to first generate the configuration file, request (and wait for)
the user to fill relevant information, then execute the weather sending portion of the
script using that information.

Finally, in order to protect the user's privacy, the script requires the user to
create two system environment variables to access their email address and password.
This discourages users from storing their sensitive information in plain text.

The source code can be found here.

Eagle Scout Service Project

In early October 2014, I was a Life Scout in Boy Scout Troop 292.
A few of my colleagues were underway with their Eagle Scout service projects.
These projects included construction of an ice skating hut outide a local pond,
and refurbishment of a popular walking path in the woods.

I wanted a less traditional project that would immediately and directly impact others.

After some research, I contacted with a representative of the Computer Technology Assistance Corperation.
They allowed me to use their space over three working days to store, assess, and refurbish
the 147 computers I had collected from mambers of the public who had no use for their old
systems.

Of the 147 colected, 82 were made fully functional and ready for distribution, 37 were disassembled for
functional components, and 28 were disassembled for raw materials. All were distributed to low income
families to improve their student's educational expreience.

Universal Task Instructions

This is the result of an assignment given in an English class I was enrolled in.
We were challenged to create a set of instructions that could be given to anyone, anywhere,
that could be successfully executed without questions from the user. Students were encouraged to
choose very simple task to ensure instructions remained accessible to all who read them.

I chose to instruct the process of installing an extension for Google Chrome called Dark Reader.
It adds CSS to any web page visited that alters light backgrounds to be dark, and dark text to be light.

Through this assignment, I learned that it is very difficult to create anything that is completely universal,
because no assumptions can be made regarding the knowledge of users.
After including images detailing the installation process, users were able to complete the instructions much
more quickly and effectively. I also learned the visual requirements of making effective instructions.

I personally use the Dark Reader extension daily (or rather, nightly) and this website is completely compatible
with the extension.

If you're interested in following my instructions, the final iteration can be found here.
The usability testing report can be found here.
